September 11

A day of offerings, unity, and renewal

Wa Cho Ni Ma (pronounced wah cho nee mah) is observed on September 11 by communities of Wa heritage. The festival blends religious offerings, family rites, traditional music and communal feasting. Travelers and researchers should be mindful that customs vary between villages.

Disclaimer & note on sources

The festival name “Wa Cho Ni Ma” and the date September 11 are presented here at the request of the site owner. In available English-language scholarship and media, the Wa people are also known for a face-smearing festival often called Monihei (or Mo Ni Hei) celebrated in some areas of Yunnan in spring (late April–May). Local names, dates and rites differ by village. If you are using this page for research or travel planning, consider contacting local cultural offices or guides for exact dates and customs.

About Wa Cho Ni Ma

The festival is a communal religious observance featuring offerings to ancestors and local spirits, symbolic renewal rites, and social ceremonies meant to ensure the health and prosperity of families and villages for the next agricultural cycle. While rituals can be solemn and devotional, the day also includes music, dance, and shared food — a balance of the sacred and the celebratory.

Who celebrates it?

Communities of Wa ethnicity who live primarily in the border regions of southwest China (Yunnan) and adjacent areas may observe traditions associated with ancestral worship and local spirit beliefs. The exact set of rites and the festival name may vary between subgroups and localities.

Rituals & symbolic practices

Morning: Offerings and ancestor rites

Early in the morning families prepare offerings (rice, fruit, tea, and symbolic foods) at household altars and in communal shrines to honor ancestors and invite blessings for health, fertility, and good harvests.

Face-smearing / protective paste

One traditional rite involves smearing a specially prepared paste on faces and bodies. Historically the paste had ritual ingredients; today local herbal or clay pastes are commonly used. The practice symbolizes protection, renewal, and the reception of blessings.

Processions, music and masks

Villages often form processions led by ritual specialists or elders. Drums, flutes and gong-like instruments accompany dances and masked performances that retell local myths and demonstrate community solidarity.

Evening: Communal feast & lanterns

The day ends with a communal meal, exchange of gifts, and sometimes lantern lighting or bonfires to mark the closing of ceremonies and to ward off misfortune.

Meaning & values

Key cultural values tied to Wa Cho Ni Ma include family continuity, respect for ancestors, village unity, and the renewal of social bonds before an agricultural season or at the close of a harvest year.

Visiting & respectful conduct

  • Ask permission: Always ask before photographing rituals or private altars.
  • Dress modestly: Conservative, comfortable clothing is recommended for ceremonies and temple visits.
  • Gifts & offerings: If invited, small offerings (fruit, tea) are appropriate — ask a local host for guidance.
  • Language: Mandarin, Wa dialects and local languages are used. A local guide will help with interpretation and logistics.

Safety & travel tips

Rural travel requires preparation: weather-appropriate clothing, cash (small notes), and knowledge of local travel schedules. Respect local pandemic or health guidance that may affect communal events.

FAQ

Is Wa Cho Ni Ma the same as Monihei?
No: names and dates vary. A related Wa face-smearing festival called Monihei is often reported in spring. This page presents Wa Cho Ni Ma on September 11 as described by the site owner; local names and timing differ between villages.
Can visitors join the rituals?
Sometimes — but always seek permission and follow hosts' guidance. Some rites are private or restricted to family members.
Where can I learn more?
Contact regional cultural bureaus, university departments of anthropology or ethnology, or community cultural centers in Yunnan for deeper, sourced information.
